# Abstract
Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) is driven by complex interactions among transcription factors that regulate cell fate decisions. In this study, we employed Random Circuit Perturbation (RACIPE) to model the dynamics of a core gene regulatory network associated with AML and to investigate the emergence of phenotypic heterogeneity. Steady-state analysis revealed the presence of multiple stable transcriptional states, indicating inherent multistability within the network.

To identify key regulators, systematic knockdown simulations were performed for five transcription factors: MYC, E2F1, STAT3, TP53, and DNMT1. The impact of each perturbation was quantified using changes in phenotype distribution and Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence as a measure of network robustness. Among the factors studied, E2F1 exhibited the highest impact, significantly altering the phenotypic landscape and reducing the AML-associated state, identifying it as a central driver of proliferative behaviour. MYC showed a moderate effect, suggesting a supporting regulatory role, while TP53 perturbation altered the balance between phenotypes, highlighting its role as a key regulatory stabilizer. In contrast, STAT3 and DNMT1 knockdowns resulted in minimal changes, indicating redundancy and robustness within the network.

Overall, the results demonstrate that AML phenotypes are governed by a hierarchical regulatory structure, with a small set of key transcription factors controlling system dynamics while the network maintains resilience through compensatory mechanisms. This study highlights the importance of network-level analysis in understanding disease phenotypes and identifying potential therapeutic targets.
